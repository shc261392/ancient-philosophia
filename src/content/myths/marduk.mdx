## Overview

<Cited status="green">
**Marduk** (Sumerian: AMAR.UTU, "Calf of the Sun"; Akkadian: *Bēl*, "Lord") was the patron deity of **Babylon** and the supreme god of the Babylonian pantheon. His theological triumph — narrated in the *Enūma Eliš* ("When on High"), the Babylonian creation epic — was also a political one: Marduk's rise to supremacy among the gods mirrored and legitimized Babylon's rise to supremacy among Mesopotamian cities.<Ref id={2} />
</Cited>

## Origins

<Cited status="green">
Marduk was originally a minor local deity — the city god of a previously unimportant settlement on the Euphrates. His name (*AMAR.UTU*) connects him to the sun god Utu/Shamash. He is rarely mentioned in third-millennium texts; his meteoric rise began with the **First Dynasty of Babylon** (c. 1894–1595 BCE), particularly under **Hammurabi** (c. 1792–1750 BCE), who transformed Babylon into the dominant power of southern Mesopotamia.<Ref id={2} />
</Cited>

## The Enūma Eliš

<Cited status="green">
The ***Enūma Eliš*** — composed probably during the reign of Nebuchadnezzar I (c. 1125–1104 BCE) — is a 1,100-line epic that narrates the creation of the world through Marduk's combat with the primordial sea goddess **Tiamat**:<Ref id={2} />
</Cited>

### The Narrative
<Cited status="green">
1. In the beginning: **Apsu** (sweet water) and **Tiamat** (salt water) mingle, producing the first gods
2. The younger gods' noise disturbs Apsu. He plots to destroy them; **Ea** (Enki) kills Apsu first
3. Tiamat, enraged, creates an army of demons and monsters, led by **Qingu** — to whom she gives the **Tablet of Destinies**
4. The older gods despair. Only **Marduk** — Ea's son — volunteers to fight Tiamat
5. His condition: if victorious, Marduk will be **supreme king of all gods**. The gods agree
6. Marduk arms himself with the winds, a net, a bow, and a mace. He **traps Tiamat in the net**, drives the wind into her open mouth, and **pierces her distended belly with an arrow**
7. He splits her body in half: the upper half becomes the **sky**, the lower half the **earth**
8. From Qingu's blood, mixed with clay, Marduk creates **humanity** — to serve the gods and free them from labor
9. The gods build **Esagila** (Marduk's temple in Babylon) and bestow upon him **fifty names**, each representing a domain of cosmic authority<Ref id={2} />
</Cited>

## The Fifty Names

<Cited status="green">
The enumeration of Marduk's fifty names in Tablets VI–VII of the *Enūma Eliš* was a theological tour de force: by absorbing the names and powers of older gods (Enlil, Ea, Anu, etc.), Marduk became a comprehensive deity — not replacing the older gods but **subsuming** their attributes. Assyriology calls this **"henotheistic tendencies"** in Babylonian religion.<Ref id={2} />
</Cited>

## Esagila and the Etemenanki

<Cited status="green">
**Esagila** ("House Whose Top Is High") was Marduk's great temple in Babylon — the religious center of the Babylonian world. Adjacent to it stood the **Etemenanki** ("House of the Foundation of Heaven and Earth") — the ziggurat of Babylon, widely identified as the inspiration for the biblical **Tower of Babel** (Genesis 11:1–9).<Ref id={4} />
</Cited>

<Cited status="yellow">
Herodotus (*Histories* 1.181–183) described the Etemenanki as a tower of eight stages, with a sanctuary at the top containing a great golden couch where a priestess slept.
</Cited>

## The Akītu Festival

<Cited status="yellow">
The **Akītu** (Babylonian New Year festival), held over twelve days in the month of Nisannu (March/April), was the most important religious event in Babylonia:
</Cited>

<Cited status="yellow">
1. Recitation of the *Enūma Eliš* in its entirety before Marduk's statue
2. The king's **humiliation ritual** — the king was stripped of his insignia before Marduk's priest, slapped across the face, and forced to declare that he had not sinned against Babylon or neglected Marduk. If the king wept, Marduk was pleased
3. Marduk's statue was carried in procession along the **Processional Way** (Aī-ibūr-šabû) through the **Ishtar Gate**
4. Sacred marriage rite (*hierogamy*)
5. Determination of fates for the coming year
</Cited>

## Marduk in Exile

<Cited status="yellow">
When Babylon was conquered, Marduk's statue was sometimes carried off — the theological equivalent of the city's enslavement:
- **Hittite sack** (c. 1595 BCE) — Marduk's statue taken to Hatti
- **Elamite sack** (c. 1155 BCE) — Taken to Susa; recovered by Nebuchadnezzar I
- **Assyrian periods** — Sennacherib sacked Babylon (689 BCE) and destroyed Marduk's statue; Esarhaddon rebuilt it
</Cited>

<Cited status="yellow">
The recovery of Marduk's statue was a political and theological triumph celebrated as a cosmic restoration of order.
</Cited>
