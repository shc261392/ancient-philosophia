## Overview

<Cited status="green">
Middle Egyptian is the form of the ancient Egyptian language used during the Middle Kingdom (c. 2055–1650 BCE). It is the "classical" stage of Egyptian, analogous to Classical Latin or Attic Greek — the prestige literary and monumental language that continued to be used for religious and formal purposes long after it ceased to be a spoken vernacular. The vast majority of Egypt's most famous texts — including the Pyramid Texts' later editions,  temple inscriptions, and literary masterpieces — were written in Middle Egyptian.<Ref id={1} />
</Cited>

## Writing System

<Cited status="green">
Middle Egyptian was written in three scripts:
- **Hieroglyphic** — The monumental script carved on temples and tombs; a mixture of logographic, syllabic, and alphabetic signs
- **Hieratic** — The cursive "priestly" script used on papyrus for administrative and literary texts
- **The Egyptian "alphabet"** — A subset of 24 uniliteral signs that represent single consonants; students typically learn these first<Ref id={1} />
</Cited>

<Cited status="yellow">
Egyptian writing did not normally indicate vowels, so the pronunciation of ancient Egyptian is reconstructed through Coptic (the final stage of the language, written in a Greek-based alphabet with vowels) and other evidence.<Ref id={1} />
</Cited>

## Grammar Highlights

<Cited status="yellow">
- **Triconsonantal roots** — Like other Afroasiatic languages, Egyptian words are based on consonantal roots (typically 2–3 consonants)
- **Verb forms** — The *sḏm.f* ("he hears") construction is the basic verbal form; *sḏm.n.f* for past; *sḏm.ḫr.f* for obligation
- **Sentence structure** — Primarily Verb-Subject-Object (VSO)
- **Determinatives** — Silent signs at the end of words that indicate semantic category (human, plant, abstract concept, etc.)<Ref id={1} />
</Cited>

## Key Texts for Students

<Cited status="green">
- **The Story of Sinuhe** — The masterpiece of Middle Egyptian literature; many grammars use it for reading practice
- **The Eloquent Peasant** — Nine speeches on social justice
- **The Shipwrecked Sailor** — A tale of adventure and magic
- **Stela of Amenemhat** — A widely used practice text for beginners<Ref id={1} />
</Cited>

## Recommended Learning Path

<Cited status="yellow">
1. **Learn the hieroglyphic signs** (start with the 24 uniliterals, then common biliterals and triliterals)
2. **Work through a grammar** (Allen or Hoch — see below)
3. **Read simple stelae and offering formulae**
4. **Progress to literary texts** (Sinuhe, Eloquent Peasant)
5. **Practice with the TLA digital corpus**
</Cited>

## Learning Resources

### Textbooks & Grammars
- **James P. Allen, *Middle Egyptian: An Introduction to the Language and Culture of Hieroglyphs*** (3rd ed., Cambridge) — The most widely used textbook; excellent and comprehensive
- **James E. Hoch, *Middle Egyptian Grammar*** — Popular alternative, especially in North American universities
- **Antonio Loprieno, *Ancient Egyptian: A Linguistic Introduction*** — Scholarly linguistic overview of all stages
- **Alan Gardiner, *Egyptian Grammar*** (3rd ed.) — Classic reference grammar, still the most complete sign list

### Online Resources
- **[Thesaurus Linguae Aegyptiae (TLA)](https://thesaurus-linguae-aegyptiae.de/)** — The principal digital corpus of Egyptian texts
- **[JSesh](https://jsesh.qenherkhopeshef.org/)** — Free hieroglyphic text editor
- **[Digital Egypt for Universities (UCL)](https://www.ucl.ac.uk/museums-static/digitalegypt/)** — Encyclopedic resource
- **[UCLA Encyclopedia of Egyptology](https://uee.cdh.ucla.edu/)** — Peer-reviewed scholarly articles
- **[Sign List (Gardiner's)](https://www.hierogl.ch/hiero/Thot)** — Online hieroglyphic sign dictionary

### Courses & Communities
- Many universities offer Middle Egyptian (check Egyptology departments at Chicago, Yale, Oxford, Leiden, Berlin)
- **r/AncientEgyptian** — Reddit community for learners
- YouTube: **Dr. Foy Scalf** (Oriental Institute) and others offer introductory lectures
