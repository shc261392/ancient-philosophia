## Overview

Akkadian is the earliest attested Semitic language, written in **cuneiform** (wedge-shaped) script on clay tablets. It was the lingua franca of the ancient Near East from the late third millennium through the first millennium BCE, used for international diplomacy (the Amarna Letters), literature (the Epic of Gilgamesh), law (Code of Hammurabi), medicine, astronomy, and administration. The two main dialects are **Babylonian** (southern) and **Assyrian** (northern).

## Writing System

Akkadian adopted the cuneiform script from the Sumerians and adapted it for a very different kind of language:
- **Syllabic signs** — Most signs represent syllables (CV, VC, or CVC): *ba, ab, bab*
- **Logograms (Sumerograms)** — Many Sumerian word-signs were retained for their meaning: LUGAL for "king" (*šarrum*)
- **Determinatives** — Semantic classifiers (similar to Egyptian determinatives): DINGIR for divinities, KUR for lands
- The sign inventory runs to several hundred commonly used signs

## Grammar Highlights

As a Semitic language, Akkadian shares features with Arabic and Hebrew:
- **Triconsonantal root system** — e.g., the root Š-P-R yields *šipru* (message), *šāpiru* (governor), *šapāru* (to send)
- **Case system** — Nominative (-*um*), accusative (-*am*), genitive (-*im*) in earlier periods
- **Verb system** — G-stem (basic), D-stem (intensive), Š-stem (causative), N-stem (passive); preterite, durative, perfect, stative
- **Mimation and nunation** — Case endings with *-m* or *-n* that gradually disappeared over time
- **Ventive** — A special verbal suffix (-*am*, -*nim*) indicating motion toward the speaker

**Standard Babylonian** — A literary dialect used across Mesopotamia for prestigious texts (e.g., the standard version of Gilgamesh)

## Key Texts for Students

- **Code of Hammurabi** — Well-edited and well-studied; excellent for learning Old Babylonian
- **Epic of Gilgamesh** — The masterpiece of Akkadian literature (Standard Babylonian)
- **Enuma Elish** — The Babylonian creation epic
- **Amarna Letters** — Diplomatic correspondence, showing the peripheral Akkadian of the Levant
- **Neo-Assyrian Royal Inscriptions** — Rich source for Neo-Assyrian dialect

## Recommended Learning Path

1. **Learn the cuneiform sign inventory** (start with ~100 most common signs)
2. **Work through a grammar** (Huehnergard — see below)
3. **Practice reading edited texts** in normalized transcription before attempting cuneiform directly
4. **Read the Code of Hammurabi** (extensively edited with sign-by-sign commentary available)
5. **Progress to literary texts** (Gilgamesh, Enuma Elish)

## Learning Resources

### Textbooks & Grammars
- **John Huehnergard, *A Grammar of Akkadian*** (3rd ed., Harvard Semitic Studies) — The standard English-language textbook; used at most American and many European universities
- **John Huehnergard, *An Introduction to Akkadian*** — Companion reader
- **Wolfram von Soden, *Grundriss der Akkadischen Grammatik*** — The definitive reference grammar (in German)
- **Jeremy Black, Andrew George & Nicholas Postgate (eds.), *A Concise Dictionary of Akkadian*** (CDA) — Essential student dictionary

### Online Resources
- **[ORACC](http://oracc.museum.upenn.edu/)** — Open Richly Annotated Cuneiform Corpus; many text editions with glossaries
- **[CDLI](https://cdli.mpiwg-berlin.mpg.de/)** — Cuneiform Digital Library Initiative
- **[ePSD2](http://oracc.museum.upenn.edu/epsd2/)** — Electronic Pennsylvania Sumerian/Akkadian Dictionary
- **[RINAP](http://oracc.museum.upenn.edu/rinap/)** — Royal Inscriptions of the Neo-Assyrian Period
- **[Cuneify](https://cuneify.herokuapp.com/)** — Online cuneiform sign lookup tool
