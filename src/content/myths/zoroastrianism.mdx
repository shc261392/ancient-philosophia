## Overview

<Cited status="red">
Zoroastrianism is one of the world's oldest continuously practiced religions, founded on the teachings of the prophet **Zarathustra** (Greek: Zoroaster), whose date remains debated — estimates range from c. 1500 BCE to c. 600 BCE. At its core stands a radical ethical dualism: the cosmic struggle between **Ahura Mazda** (the Wise Lord, truth and light) and **Angra Mainyu** (the Destructive Spirit, falsehood and darkness). This struggle pervades all creation, and every human being is called to choose sides.
</Cited>

<Cited status="red">
Zoroastrianism was the dominant religion of the Achaemenid, Parthian, and Sasanian empires, shaping Iranian civilization for over a millennium. Its concepts — heaven and hell, final judgment, the resurrection of the dead, a messianic savior — profoundly influenced Judaism, Christianity, and Islam.
</Cited>

## Core Theology

### Ahura Mazda
<Cited status="red">
The supreme, uncreated god — wise, just, and wholly good. Ahura Mazda created the world as a battlefield against evil, and will ultimately triumph. See also: [Ahura Mazda](/myths/ahura-mazda/).
</Cited>

### Angra Mainyu (Ahriman)
<Cited status="red">
The destructive spirit, source of evil, disease, and death. Not a co-equal god but a lesser being who chose falsehood (*druj*) and will eventually be defeated. The dualism is ethical then, not metaphysical — Ahura Mazda is ultimately supreme.
</Cited>

### Asha and Druj
<Cited status="red">
The central ethical opposition: **asha** (truth, cosmic order, righteousness) vs. **druj** (the Lie, chaos, wickedness). Every thought, word, and deed either upholds asha or serves druj. This binary moral framework is the foundation of Zoroastrian ethics.
</Cited>

## The Amesha Spentas

<Cited status="red">
The "Bounteous Immortals" — seven divine emanations of Ahura Mazda, each associated with an aspect of creation:
</Cited>

<Cited status="red">
| Amesha Spenta | Domain | Creation |
|--------------|--------|----------|
| Vohu Manah | Good Mind | Cattle |
| Asha Vahishta | Best Truth | Fire |
| Khshathra Vairya | Desirable Dominion | Metals |
| Spenta Armaiti | Holy Devotion | Earth |
| Haurvatat | Wholeness | Water |
| Ameretat | Immortality | Plants |
| (Ahura Mazda) | (Wise Lord) | Humanity |
</Cited>

## The Yazatas

<Cited status="red">
Below the Amesha Spentas are the **Yazatas** ("Worthy of Worship") — a large class of divine beings including **Mithra** (oath and covenant), **Anahita** (waters and fertility), **Atar** (sacred fire), and **Sraosha** (obedience, protector of the righteous dead). Mithra and Anahita were particularly important in popular worship and Achaemenid royal inscriptions.
</Cited>

## The Gathas

<Cited status="red">
The **Gathas** are seventeen hymns attributed to Zarathustra himself, composed in an archaic form of Avestan closely related to Vedic Sanskrit. They are the oldest and most sacred part of the **Avesta** (the Zoroastrian scripture). The Gathas are philosophical, poetic, and sometimes obscure — they address Ahura Mazda directly, wrestle with the problem of evil, and call for righteous action.
</Cited>

<Cited status="red">
The wider Avesta includes the **Yasna** (liturgical texts), **Yashts** (hymns to individual Yazatas), and the **Vendidad** (ritual purity laws).
</Cited>

## Fire Temples

<Cited status="red">
Fire is the sacred symbol of asha — truth and divine light. Zoroastrian worship centered on the **fire temple** (*atash kadeh*), where a sacred fire was maintained perpetually. Three grades of fire existed: the highest, **Atash Bahram** ("Fire of Victory"), required elaborate consecration from sixteen different source fires. Zoroastrians do not worship fire itself — they pray in its presence as a symbol of Ahura Mazda's light.
</Cited>

## Eschatology

<Cited status="red">
Zoroastrian eschatology is remarkably detailed and was enormously influential:
</Cited>

<Cited status="red">
- **Individual judgment** — After death, the soul crosses the **Chinvat Bridge**; for the righteous, it is wide and leads to paradise; for the wicked, it narrows to a razor's edge
- **Heaven, hell, and purgatory** (*hamistagan*) — Destinations determined by the balance of good and evil deeds
- **The Saoshyant** — A future savior born of Zarathustra's lineage who will lead the final battle against evil
- **Frashokereti** — The "Making Wonderful," the final renovation of the world when evil is permanently defeated, the dead are resurrected, and creation is restored to perfection
</Cited>

## Achaemenid Royal Religion

<Cited status="red">
The Achaemenid kings (c. 550–330 BCE) proclaimed Ahura Mazda as their patron. Darius I's great inscription at Bisitun declares: "By the favor of Ahura Mazda I am king." The royal tombs at Naqsh-e Rostam show the king before a fire altar beneath the winged symbol of Ahura Mazda (or the royal *fravashi*). However, the exact relationship between Achaemenid religion and the Zoroastrianism of the Gathas remains debated among scholars.
</Cited>

## Influence on Later Religions

<Cited status="red">
Zoroastrian concepts that influenced Judaism, Christianity, and Islam include:
</Cited>

<Cited status="red">
- Cosmic dualism (good vs. evil)
- Angels and demons
- Heaven and hell
- Bodily resurrection
- Final judgment
- A messianic savior figure
- Linear eschatology (history moving toward a final resolution)
</Cited>

<Cited status="red">
These parallels emerged particularly after the Jewish exile in Babylon (586 BCE), when the Israelites came under Achaemenid rule.
</Cited>

## Primary Sources

- **The Gathas** (Yasna 28–34, 43–51, 53) — Zarathustra's own hymns
- **Yasna Haptanghaiti** (Yasna 35–41) — Archaic liturgical texts
- **Yashts** — Hymns celebrating individual Yazatas (esp. Mithra Yasht, Anahita Yasht)
- **Vendidad** — Ritual purity law
- **Achaemenid royal inscriptions** — Darius at Bisitun, Xerxes' *Daiva* inscription
- **Bundahishn** — Middle Persian (Pahlavi) cosmogonic text
