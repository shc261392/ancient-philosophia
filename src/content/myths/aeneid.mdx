## Overview

<Cited status="red">
The **Aeneid** (*Aeneis*), composed by **Publius Vergilius Maro** (Virgil, 70–19 BCE), is the great literary epic of Rome and one of the most influential poems in Western literature. Written during the reign of Augustus, the Aeneid tells the story of the Trojan hero **Aeneas**, who escapes the destruction of Troy, wanders the Mediterranean, and ultimately founds the settlement in Latium that will become Rome.
</Cited>

<Cited status="red">
Unlike the Homeric epics, which emerged from oral tradition, the Aeneid is a **literary epic** — self-consciously modeled on Homer but fundamentally different in conception. It is a poem about destiny, duty (*pietas*), and the cost of empire. Virgil died before completing his final revisions and reportedly asked that the manuscript be burned. Augustus overruled this request, and the Aeneid became the foundational text of Roman cultural identity.
</Cited>

## Structure

<Cited status="red">
The Aeneid comprises twelve books, deliberately echoing both Homeric epics:
</Cited>

### The Odyssean Half (Books 1–6)
<Cited status="red">
The first half mirrors the Odyssey — a hero wandering the sea, driven by divine hostility, seeking a destined homeland.
</Cited>

<Cited status="red">
**Book 1** — Storm and shipwreck; arrival at Carthage; Dido welcomes the Trojans
**Book 2** — Aeneas narrates the fall of Troy: the Wooden Horse, the death of Priam, the flight carrying his father Anchises and leading his son Ascanius, the loss of his wife Creusa
**Book 3** — Wanderings through the Mediterranean: Thrace, Delos, Crete, the Harpies, Buthrotum (where Helenus and Andromache have rebuilt a miniature Troy)
**Book 4** — **Dido and Aeneas** — The central tragedy of the first half. Dido, queen of Carthage, and Aeneas become lovers. Jupiter sends Mercury to recall Aeneas to his destiny. Aeneas departs; Dido kills herself on a funeral pyre, cursing Rome and prophesying Hannibal. This book is among the most powerful in Latin literature.
**Book 5** — Funeral games for Anchises in Sicily
**Book 6** — **The Descent to the Underworld** (*katabasis*) — Guided by the Sibyl of Cumae, Aeneas enters the underworld through Lake Avernus. He encounters Dido's shade (she turns away in silence), meets his father Anchises, and witnesses the **Parade of Heroes** — future Roman leaders from Romulus to Augustus, awaiting birth. Anchises reveals Rome's destiny: *tu regere imperio populos, Romane, memento* — "Remember, Roman, to rule the peoples with your power."
</Cited>

### The Iliadic Half (Books 7–12)
<Cited status="red">
The second half mirrors the Iliad — war in Italy, aristeia, single combat, and the cost of conquest.
</Cited>

<Cited status="red">
**Book 7** — Arrival in Latium; King Latinus offers his daughter Lavinia, but Juno incites the Rutulian prince **Turnus** and the Latin queen Amata to resist
**Books 8–9** — Aeneas allies with Evander (on the future site of Rome) and the Etruscans; Turnus attacks the Trojan camp; the episode of Nisus and Euryalus
**Book 10** — Full-scale war; death of young Pallas (Evander's son) at Turnus's hands
**Book 11** — Funeral of Pallas; the warrior maiden Camilla
**Book 12** — **Single combat between Aeneas and Turnus** — Turnus is defeated. He begs for mercy. Aeneas hesitates — then sees Pallas's sword-belt on Turnus and, consumed by fury (*furiis accensus*), kills him. The poem ends abruptly with this act of violent rage, leaving the reader to wrestle with its meaning.
</Cited>

## Literary Epic vs. Oral Tradition

<Cited status="red">
The Aeneid belongs to a fundamentally different literary mode than the Homeric poems:
</Cited>

<Cited status="red">
- **Self-conscious artistry** — Every line is crafted; Virgil reportedly wrote only a few lines per day
- **Intertextuality** — Constant allusion to Homer, Apollonius of Rhodes, Ennius, and other predecessors
- **Political dimension** — The poem legitimates Augustus's regime by tracing Roman destiny from Troy
- **Ambiguity** — The poem can be read as triumphant celebration of Rome *or* as a dark meditation on the violence inherent in empire
</Cited>

## Themes

<Cited status="red">
- ***Pietas*** — Duty to gods, family, and destiny; Aeneas is *pius Aeneas*, defined by obedience to divine will, even at personal cost
- **The cost of empire** — The Aeneid is haunted by suffering: Dido, Turnus, Pallas, Lausus, Camilla — the poem asks whether Rome's greatness is worth the blood it requires
- ***Fatum*** (fate) — Destiny drives the narrative; individual will is subordinated to the divine plan
- **Memory and loss** — Aeneas carries Troy with him — its gods (the Penates), its memory, its trauma
- **Augustus and ideology** — The poem links Augustus to Aeneas, Jupiter's prophecy to Roman history
</Cited>

## Primary Sources

- **Virgil, *Aeneid*** — The poem itself, preserved in excellent manuscript tradition; oldest fragments in the *Schedae Vaticanae* (4th–5th century CE)
- **Servius, *Commentary on the Aeneid*** — The most important ancient commentary (4th century CE)
- **Macrobius, *Saturnalia*** — Discusses Virgil's use of Homer and earlier Latin poets
- **Donatus, *Vita Vergilii*** — Ancient biography of Virgil
