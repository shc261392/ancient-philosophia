## Overview

Luwian is an **Anatolian Indo-European language**, closely related to Hittite. It was widely spoken across western and southern Anatolia during the Bronze Age and survived into the Iron Age in the **Neo-Hittite/Syro-Hittite** kingdoms of southeastern Turkey and northern Syria. Luwian is notable for being written in **two distinct scripts**: cuneiform (borrowed from Hittite scribal tradition) and a unique **hieroglyphic** system (Anatolian Hieroglyphs) used on rock reliefs, seals, and monumental inscriptions.

## Two Scripts

- **Cuneiform Luwian** — Used in the Hittite capital Hattusa; embedded in Hittite texts as ritual passages
- **Hieroglyphic Luwian** — A logosyllabic script of ~500 signs used on stone monuments, rock reliefs, lead strips, and seals; flourished especially in the Neo-Hittite kingdoms (c. 1200–700 BCE) after the fall of the Hittite Empire

## Key Features

- **i-mutation** — A characteristic Luwian sound change
- **-ssa/-zza suffixes** — Used to form adjectives (compare Hittite -ssa-)
- **Quotative particle *-wa-***  — Marks direct speech in hieroglyphic texts
- **Close to Hittite** — But with distinct phonology, morphology, and vocabulary

## Sample Text

From the Karatepe Bilingual (8th century BCE, Hieroglyphic Luwian version):

> |EGO-mi MAGNUS.REX Azatiwatas HEROS |URBS+ra/i-wa/i-ni-sa-za(URBS) REX

*amu=wa=mi ura-wani-sa-nza LUGAL Azatiwata-sa tara/i-wani-sa*

"I am Azatiwatas, the Sun-blessed, king of the Danunians."

This Phoenician-Luwian bilingual inscription was vital for confirming the decipherment of Hieroglyphic Luwian.

## Key Inscriptions

- **Karatepe Bilingual** — The most important Luwian-Phoenician bilingual text (8th century BCE)
- **ALEPPO 6** — Temple inscription at the Storm-God temple in Aleppo
- **TOPADA** — Long rock inscription from central Anatolia
- **Silver Bowl of Bohça** — Inscribed metal vessel
