## Overview

<Cited status="red">
Linear B is a syllabic writing system used to write **Mycenaean Greek**, the earliest known form of Greek. The tablets were found at palatial centers in Crete (Knossos) and mainland Greece (Pylos, Mycenae, Thebes, Tiryns). Michael **Ventris** deciphered Linear B in 1952 with help from John Chadwick, proving that the Mycenaean palaces were Greek-speaking — a revelation that fundamentally changed our understanding of Greek prehistory.
</Cited>

## The Writing System

<Cited status="red">
- **~87 syllabic signs** — Each sign represents a consonant-vowel (CV) syllable: *pa, pe, pi, po, pu*, etc.
- **Logograms/ideograms** — Pictographic signs for commodities (wheat, olive oil, horses, chariots, textiles)
- **Number system** — Decimal, with signs for 1, 10, 100, 1000
- **Limitations:** Cannot represent consonant clusters, final consonants, or distinguish /r/ from /l/ — so a single sign can represent multiple sounds
</Cited>

## Mycenaean Greek Features

<Cited status="red">
- **Labiovelars preserved** — *qe* for the labiovelar /kʷ/ that became π, τ, or κ in later Greek
- **Digamma preserved** — /w/ still present: *wo-i-ko* = οἶκος "house"
- **Case system** — Preserves the instrumental case lost in later Greek
- **Vocabulary** — Many familiar Greek words: *wa-na-ka* = ἄναξ "lord", *pa-te* = πατήρ "father"
</Cited>

## Sample Text

<Cited status="red">
Pylos tablet PY Eb 297.1 (land allocation record):
</Cited>

<Cited status="red">
> <span class="text-2xl leading-relaxed">𐀁𐀐𐀆𐀃 𐀏𐀐𐀍𐀙 𐀲𐀪𐀊</span>
>
> *i-je-re-ja e-ke-qe te-me-no e-ke-qe*
> *to-so-de pe-mo GRA 3*
>
> *hiereia ekhei-kʷe temenos ekhei-kʷe; toson-de spermo GRA 3*
>
> "The priestess holds, and she holds a temenos (sacred plot); so much seed: 3 (units of) wheat."
</Cited>

<Cited status="red">
This tablet is one of many economic records documenting land tenure, agricultural production, and religious personnel.
</Cited>

## Key Discoveries

<Cited status="red">
- **Knossos tablets** — ~3,000 tablets from Arthur Evans's excavations (written c. 1375 BCE)
- **Pylos tablets** — ~1,200 tablets from Carl Blegen's excavations (written c. 1200 BCE, just before destruction)
- **Thebes tablets** — Important finds including ritual texts
- **Mycenae and Tiryns** — Smaller but significant collections
</Cited>
