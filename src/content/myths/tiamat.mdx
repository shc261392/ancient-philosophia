## Overview

<Cited status="green">
**Tiamat** (Akkadian: *ti'āmtum*, "sea") was the primordial salt-water ocean in Babylonian cosmology — the feminine cosmic principle whose body, split by the champion god **Marduk**, became heaven and earth. She is the central antagonist of the *Enūma Eliš*, the Babylonian creation epic, and one of the most powerful mythological figures in the ancient Near East: simultaneously the **mother of all gods** and the **dragon of chaos** who must be slain for the ordered world to exist.<Ref id={1} />
</Cited>

## The Primordial State

<Cited status="yellow">
According to the *Enūma Eliš* (Tablet I):<Ref id={2} />
</Cited>

<Cited status="yellow">
> *"When on high the heaven had not been named, below the firm ground had not been called by name... Tiamat mingled their waters together with Apsu."*<Ref id={3} />
</Cited>

<Cited status="yellow">
Before creation, two bodies of water existed:
- **Apsu** — The primordial sweet-water ocean (masculine)
- **Tiamat** — The primordial salt-water ocean (feminine)<Ref id={1} />
</Cited>

<Cited status="yellow">
From their **mingling** (*ḫe-pu*, a word also used for sexual intercourse), the first gods were born: **Lahmu and Lahamu** → **Anshar and Kishar** → **Anu** → **Ea/Enki**.<Ref id={2} />
</Cited>

<Cited status="yellow">
Tiamat was thus the ultimate ancestress — the mother (or grandmother) of all deities.<Ref id={3} />
</Cited>

## The Conflict

<Cited status="yellow">
The younger gods' activity and noise disturbed Apsu. He proposed destroying them; Tiamat objected (*"Why should we destroy what we have created?"*). But Ea preemptively killed Apsu.<Ref id={1} />
</Cited>

<Cited status="yellow">
Tiamat, initially reluctant to fight her own descendants, was eventually goaded into war by the **older gods** who resented the younger generation. She raised an army:<Ref id={2} />
</Cited>

### Tiamat's Monstrous Legion
<Cited status="yellow">
- **Eleven monsters** created from her body: viper, sharp-tooth dragon, great lion, mad dog, scorpion-man, fish-man, and others
- **Qingu** appointed as her general and lover; she gave him the **Tablet of Destinies**
- This army terrified all the older gods — none dared face it<Ref id={3} />
</Cited>

## The Battle

<Cited status="green">
Only **Marduk** accepted the challenge. The combat is described in Tablet IV with cinematic intensity:<Ref id={1} />
</Cited>

<Cited status="red">
1. Marduk and Tiamat exchange challenges — she opens her mouth to swallow him
2. Marduk drives the **four winds** into her mouth, inflating her body
3. He shoots an **arrow** through her distended belly — it splits her heart
4. Tiamat's army scatters; Qingu is captured; the Tablet of Destinies seized
</Cited>

## The Creation from Tiamat's Body

<Cited status="red">
This is the cosmogonic moment. Marduk **splits Tiamat's corpse** "like a dried fish" into two halves:
</Cited>

<Cited status="red">
- The **upper half** becomes the sky (held up by a bar to keep the waters above from falling)
- The **lower half** becomes the earth
- Her eyes become the sources of the **Tigris and Euphrates**
- Her tail becomes the **Milky Way** (according to some interpretations)
- Her breasts become **mountains**
</Cited>

<Cited status="yellow">
The world, in Babylonian cosmology, is literally made of the body of the defeated chaos goddess. The sky is Tiamat above; the earth is Tiamat below; the cosmic order is the dismembered chaos.
</Cited>

## Theological Significance

### Chaoskampf
<Cited status="yellow">
Tiamat's combat with Marduk belongs to the **chaoskampf** ("chaos-battle") pattern found across ancient Near Eastern and Mediterranean myths:
- **Baal vs. Yamm** (Ugaritic)
- **Yahweh vs. Leviathan / Rahab / Tehom** (Hebrew Bible — *tehom* in Genesis 1:2 is cognate with *ti'āmtum*)
- **Zeus vs. Typhon** (Greek)
- **Indra vs. Vritra** (Vedic)
</Cited>

### Gender and Chaos
<Cited status="yellow">
Tiamat as feminine chaos vs. Marduk as masculine order has attracted extensive feminist and gender-studies analysis. Some scholars argue the *Enūma Eliš* narrates the "conquest of the feminine" — a theological justification for patriarchal order. Others note that Tiamat is initially depicted sympathetically and only becomes monstrous when provoked.
</Cited>

### Tiamat and Tehom
<Cited status="yellow">
Hebrew *tehom* (תְּהוֹם, "the deep") in Genesis 1:2 — *"and darkness was upon the face of the deep"* — is linguistically cognate with Akkadian *ti'āmtum*. Unlike Tiamat, however, *tehom* in Genesis is not personified or combated — suggesting a deliberate **demythologization** of the Babylonian creation tradition by the Hebrew authors.
</Cited>

## Iconography

<Cited status="yellow">
Tiamat's visual representation is uncertain — the *Enūma Eliš* never precisely describes her form. Scholarly interpretations include:
- A **serpentine dragon** (the *mušḫuššu* or similar creature)
- A vast, formless **ocean** with no fixed shape
- The monsters she creates may be projections of her own chaotic nature
</Cited>

<Cited status="red">
The famous *mušḫuššu* dragon on the **Ishtar Gate** of Babylon is sometimes associated with Tiamat, though it is more properly Marduk's own symbol.
</Cited>
