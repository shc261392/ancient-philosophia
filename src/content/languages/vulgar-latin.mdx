## Overview

<Cited status="red">
Vulgar Latin (*sermo vulgaris*) refers to the spoken, colloquial forms of Latin used by ordinary people across the Roman Empire, as distinct from the polished literary language of Cicero and Virgil. It is not a single dialect but a spectrum of regional and social varieties whose divergence over centuries gave rise to the **Romance languages**: French, Spanish, Italian, Portuguese, Romanian, Catalan, Occitan, and others. Evidence for Vulgar Latin comes from graffiti, private letters, legal documents, Christian texts, and "mistakes" in formal inscriptions.
</Cited>

## Key Differences from Classical Latin

<Cited status="red">
- **Case system collapse** — The six-case system reduced to two or three; prepositions replaced case functions
- **Loss of neuter gender** — Neuter nouns merged with masculine
- **Analytic verb forms** — Synthetic futures and passives replaced by periphrases: *cantare habeo* → French *chanterai* ("I will sing")
- **Vowel changes** — The 10-vowel system (5 long + 5 short) collapsed into 7 vowels based on quality rather than length
- **h-dropping** — The sound /h/ was lost early: *habere* → *avere*
- **Word order** — Shift from SOV to SVO
</Cited>

## Sample Text

<Cited status="red">
Pompeii wall graffiti (CIL IV, 5296):
</Cited>

<Cited status="red">

> bis tanto pereat quisquis amare vetat

<Cited status="red">
"Let everyone who loves prosper; let him who does not know how to love perish; and twice let him perish who forbids love."
</Cited>

<Cited status="red">
"I am amazed, O wall, that you have not collapsed, bearing the tedious scribbles of so many writers."
</Cited>

## Key Sources

<Cited status="red">
- **Pompeii graffiti** — Thousands of informal inscriptions preserved by Vesuvius (79 CE)
- **Appendix Probi** — A 3rd/4th-century grammar correction list ("say X, not Y") revealing common speech
- **Peregrinatio Aetheriae** — A 4th-century pilgrim's travel diary in popular Latin
- **Gregory of Tours, *Historia Francorum*** — 6th-century history written in Merovingian Latin
- **Curse tablets (defixiones)** — Lead tablets with colloquial language
</Cited>
