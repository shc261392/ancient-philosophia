## Overview

The eruption of Mount Vesuvius on August 24, 79 CE buried the prosperous Roman towns of **Pompeii** and **Herculaneum** under meters of ash and pumice, preserving an extraordinary snapshot of Roman daily life. Since excavations began in the 18th century, these sites have yielded:

- **Architecture** — Complete Roman houses (*domus*), apartment blocks (*insulae*), shops (*tabernae*), bathhouses, temples, a gladiatorial amphitheater, and the civil forum
- **Wall Paintings** — Thousands of frescoes showing mythology, landscapes, still lifes, erotica, and daily scenes — the largest surviving corpus of Roman painting
- **Graffiti and Inscriptions** — Over 11,000 inscriptions including personal messages, election slogans, love poems, and crude jokes — invaluable for studying **Vulgar Latin**
- **Objects** — Bread carbonized in ovens, surgical instruments, gladiator helmets, jewelry, and furniture

## The Herculaneum Papyri

Hundreds of **carbonized papyrus scrolls** from the Villa of the Papyri at Herculaneum are being read using **X-ray CT scanning and AI** — a revolutionary project that may recover lost Greek philosophical texts, including works of Epicurus and Philodemus.

## Visiting

Pompeii and Herculaneum are near Naples, Italy. Both have entrance fees. The **Naples National Archaeological Museum (MANN)** houses the finest portable finds (frescoes, mosaics, Alexander Mosaic, Farnese collection).
