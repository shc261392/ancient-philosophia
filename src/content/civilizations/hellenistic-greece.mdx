## Overview

<Cited status="green">
The **Hellenistic period** began with Alexander the Great's death in **323 BCE** and ended, by convention, with the Roman conquest of Greece (**146 BCE**, destruction of Corinth) or the death of Cleopatra VII and the annexation of Egypt (**30 BCE**). It was an era in which Greek language and culture spread from the Mediterranean to Central Asia, producing a cosmopolitan civilization that fused Greek, Egyptian, Persian, and Mesopotamian traditions.<Ref id={1} />
</Cited>

<Cited status="yellow">
The word "Hellenistic" (Ἑλληνιστικός) was coined by the 19th-century historian Johann Gustav Droysen to describe this world of cultural mixing, distinguishing it from the "Hellenic" (purely Greek) civilization of the Classical period.
</Cited>

## The Wars of the Diadochi (323–281 BCE)

<Cited status="green">
Alexander died in Babylon in June 323 BCE leaving no viable heir. His generals — the **Diadochi** (Διάδοχοι, "Successors") — fought five devastating wars over his empire, eventually splitting it into rival kingdoms:<Ref id={2} />
</Cited>

<Cited status="yellow">
- **Ptolemaic Egypt** — Founded by **Ptolemy I Soter**, controlling Egypt, Cyrenaica, and Cyprus
- **Seleucid Empire** — Founded by **Seleucus I Nicator**, controlling Syria, Mesopotamia, Iran, and at times Anatolia
- **Antigonid Macedonia** — Founded by descendants of **Antigonus I Monophthalmus**, controlling Macedonia and (nominally) Greece
- **Attalid Pergamum** — A later breakaway kingdom in western Anatolia, founded by the Attalid dynasty
</Cited>

## The Hellenistic World

### Alexandria

<Cited status="green">
**Alexandria**, founded by Alexander in 331 BCE, became the cultural capital of the Hellenistic world under the Ptolemies. Its two defining institutions shaped intellectual history:<Ref id={3} />
</Cited>

<Cited status="yellow">
- **The Mouseion** (Museum, "Temple of the Muses") — A state-funded research institution where scholars lived and worked. It has been called the first university
- **The Library of Alexandria** — The ancient world's greatest collection of texts, reportedly holding 400,000–700,000 scroll-volumes at its peak. The Ptolemies pursued books aggressively, allegedly confiscating texts from ships in Alexandria's harbor
</Cited>

<Cited status="green">
Scholars working at the Mouseion included **Euclid** (geometry), **Eratosthenes** (who calculated the Earth's circumference to remarkable accuracy), **Aristarchus** (who proposed a heliocentric model), and **Callimachus** (poet and first librarian).<Ref id={4} />
</Cited>

### Science and Technology

<Cited status="yellow">
The Hellenistic period was the golden age of ancient science:
</Cited>

<Cited status="green">
- **Archimedes of Syracuse** (c. 287–212 BCE) — Mathematics, mechanics, hydrostatics; the Archimedean screw
- **Hipparchus** (c. 190–120 BCE) — Trigonometry, star catalogs, the precession of the equinoxes
- **Hero of Alexandria** (c. 10–70 CE) — The aeolipile (first steam engine), automated devices
- **Ctesibius** — Hydraulics, the water clock, the pneumatic organ<Ref id={5} />
</Cited>

### Philosophy

<Cited status="yellow">
The Hellenistic age produced three major philosophical schools:
</Cited>

<Cited status="green">
- **Stoicism** (Zeno of Citium, c. 300 BCE) — The cosmos is rational and ordered by *logos*; virtue is living in accordance with nature; external circumstances are morally "indifferent"
- **Epicureanism** (Epicurus, c. 307 BCE) — The gods exist but do not intervene; the universe is composed of atoms; pleasure (primarily the absence of pain) is the highest good
- **Skepticism** (Pyrrho, Carneades) — Certain knowledge is impossible; suspension of judgment leads to tranquility<Ref id={6} />
</Cited>

<Cited status="yellow">
All three schools responded to the same problem: in a world without the stable city-state (*polis*) of the Classical period, how should an individual live?
</Cited>

### Koine Greek

<Cited status="green">
The Hellenistic period saw the emergence of **Koine Greek** (ἡ κοινὴ διάλεκτος, "the common dialect") as the lingua franca of the eastern Mediterranean — replacing the classical Attic, Ionic, Doric dialects with a simplified, widely-understood standard. Koine was the language of commerce, administration, the New Testament, and the Septuagint (Greek translation of the Hebrew Bible).<Ref id={7} />
</Cited>

## Cultural Fusion

<Cited status="yellow">
Hellenistic culture was defined by **syncretism** — the merging of Greek and non-Greek traditions:
</Cited>

<Cited status="yellow">
- **Greco-Egyptian religion** — The cult of Serapis, the hellenized worship of Isis
- **Greco-Buddhist art** — In Gandhara (modern Pakistan/Afghanistan), the Buddha was depicted in Greek sculptural style
- **Greek literature in new contexts** — Jewish writers composed in Greek (Philo of Alexandria, Josephus)
</Cited>

## Decline and Roman Conquest

<Cited status="green">
The Hellenistic kingdoms were gradually absorbed by Rome:
- **Macedonia** fell after the Battle of Pydna (**168 BCE**) and became a Roman province in 146 BCE
- **Greece** was sacked after the destruction of Corinth (**146 BCE**)
- **Pergamum** was bequeathed to Rome by Attalus III (**133 BCE**)
- **Seleucid Empire** was reduced by Pompey (**63 BCE**)
- **Ptolemaic Egypt** fell with Cleopatra VII (**30 BCE**)<Ref id={8} />
</Cited>

<Cited status="yellow">
But Hellenistic culture outlived Hellenistic politics. Greek remained the lingua franca of the eastern Roman Empire for centuries. Roman art, philosophy, religion, and science were profoundly shaped by Hellenistic models. The common saying was: "Captured Greece took her fierce captor captive" (Horace, *Epistles* 2.1.156).
</Cited>
