## Overview

<Cited status="green">
**Mycenaean Greek** is the oldest attested form of the Greek language, written in the **Linear B** syllabic script and found on clay tablets at major palatial centers including **Knossos** (Crete), **Pylos** (Messenia), **Mycenae**, **Thebes**, and **Tiryns**. The tablets date from approximately **1450–1200 BCE** — over 600 years before Homer.<Ref id={5} />
</Cited>

<Cited status="green">
The decipherment of Linear B by **Michael Ventris** in **1952** was one of the great intellectual achievements of the 20th century. Ventris, an architect with no formal linguistic training, proved that the script recorded an early form of Greek — a discovery that pushed the history of the Greek language back by half a millennium.<Ref id={2} />
</Cited>

## The Decipherment

### Before Ventris

<Cited status="green">
Linear B was discovered by **Arthur Evans** during his excavations at Knossos beginning in 1900. Evans believed the script recorded a non-Greek "Minoan" language and resisted the idea that Bronze Age Crete could be Greek-speaking. For fifty years, the script remained undeciphered.<Ref id={5} />
</Cited>

<Cited status="green">
The breakthrough came from an unexpected direction. In 1939, **Carl Blegen** discovered over 600 Linear B tablets at **Pylos** on the Greek mainland — proving the script was used beyond Crete and making a Greek identification more plausible.<Ref id={5} />
</Cited>

### Ventris's Method

<Cited status="green">
Michael Ventris used a combinatorial approach:<Ref id={2} />
</Cited>

<Cited status="green">
1. He catalogued every Linear B sign and its frequency
2. He identified patterns suggesting an inflected language with word endings
3. He constructed a "grid" hypothesizing which signs shared vowels or consonants
4. He tested the grid by assigning phonetic values — and found that the resulting words were recognizably Greek<Ref id={5} />
</Cited>

<Cited status="green">
The key confirmation came when Ventris read a series of tablets from Knossos as lists of **tripod cauldrons** — the ideogram showed a tripod, and the spelling *ti-ri-po-de* corresponded to Greek *τρίποδε* ("two tripods").<Ref id={2} />
</Cited>

<Cited status="green">
Ventris published his findings with the scholar **John Chadwick** in "Evidence for Greek Dialect in the Mycenaean Archives" (*Journal of Hellenic Studies*, 1953). Tragically, Ventris died in a car accident in 1956 at age 34.<Ref id={2} />
</Cited>

## The Linear B Script

### Structure

<Cited status="green">
Linear B is a **syllabary** — each sign represents a syllable (typically a consonant + vowel), not a single sound. This makes it poorly suited for writing Greek, which has consonant clusters and final consonants that the script cannot represent.<Ref id={5} />
</Cited>

<Cited status="green">
| Linear B | Syllable | Greek Word | Meaning |
|----------|----------|------------|---------|
| 𐀀 | a | — | — |
| 𐀁 | e | — | — |
| 𐀂 | i | — | — |
| 𐀃 | o | — | — |
| 𐀄 | u | — | — |
| 𐀅 | da | — | — |
| 𐀏 | ka | — | — |
| 𐀫 | ra | — | — |<Ref id={5} />
</Cited>

### Spelling Rules (Orthographic Conventions)

<Cited status="yellow">
Linear B's syllabary forces certain conventions:
</Cited>

<Cited status="red">
- **Final consonants are omitted** — Greek *χαλκός* (khalkos, "bronze") → *ka-ko*
- **Consonant clusters are broken up** — Greek *σπέρμα* (sperma, "seed") → *pe-ma* (drops the *s-* entirely)
- **No distinction between l/r** — Both written with the same series
- **No distinction between voiced/voiceless/aspirate stops** — *ka* serves for /ga/, /ka/, /kha/
</Cited>

<Cited status="yellow">
This means many Linear B words are ambiguous — the same spelling can represent multiple Greek words.
</Cited>

## Content of the Tablets

<Cited status="red">
The tablets are almost entirely **administrative records** — inventories, tax lists, personnel records, and ration distributions for the palatial economy:
</Cited>

<Cited status="red">
- **Livestock counts** — Sheep, goats, pigs, cattle
- **Grain distributions** — Wheat and barley rations for workers
- **Personnel records** — Lists of women workers (*do-e-ra* = Greek *doelai*, "slave women")
- **Military equipment** — Chariots, armor, weapons
- **Religious offerings** — Dedications to gods including *di-wo* (Zeus), *e-ra* (Hera), *a-ta-na* (Athena), *po-se-da-o* (Poseidon), and *di-wo-nu-so* (Dionysus)
- **Land tenure** — Records of land holdings and obligations
</Cited>

<Cited status="red">
The tablets survive only because they were **accidentally fired** when the palaces burned. Unbaked clay tablets would have disintegrated long ago.
</Cited>

## Mycenaean Greek Language

### Relationship to Later Greek

<Cited status="yellow">
Mycenaean Greek is not the ancestor of any single later Greek dialect — it appears to be closest to **Arcadocypriot** Greek (the dialects of Arcadia and Cyprus), but contains features found in both East Greek (Ionic-Attic) and West Greek (Doric) branches.
</Cited>

<Cited status="yellow">
Key archaic features preserved in Mycenaean:
</Cited>

<Cited status="yellow">
- **The labiovelars** — Mycenaean preserves *qo-* (labioveral /kʷ/) where later Greek has *π-* (Attic) or *τ-* (other dialects). E.g., *qo-u-ko-ro* = later *βουκόλος* ("cowherd")
- **The digamma** — The sound /w/ (*wanax* = "lord," later *ἄναξ*)
- **Dual number** — Fully functional grammatical dual, partially lost in later Greek
</Cited>
